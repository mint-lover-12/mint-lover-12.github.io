What is an Element?

An element is a pure substance made up of only one type of atom, defined by its number of protons (atomic number, Z).

Each element has a unique atomic number. Elements are organised in the periodic table by increasing atomic number, arranged into periods (rows) and groups (columns).

Atomic Structure

Every atom has a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, with electrons orbiting in shells.

02 Periodic Trends ‚ñæ

Atomic Radius

Across a period (left ‚Üí right): atomic radius decreases ‚Äî nuclear charge increases, shielding stays constant, so electrons are pulled in closer.
Down a group: atomic radius increases ‚Äî new electron shells added, more shielding, nucleus less effective at holding outer electrons.

Ionisation Energy

First ionisation energy (IE‚ÇÅ): energy to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms.

Across a period: IE‚ÇÅ increases ‚Äî greater nuclear charge, same shielding, harder to remove electrons.
Down a group: IE‚ÇÅ decreases ‚Äî outer electrons further away and more shielded.

Electronegativity

Measure of an atom's ability to attract electrons in a bond.

Increases across a period; decreases down a group. Fluorine is the most electronegative element.

Down Group 17: atomic radius increases ‚Üí nucleus further from bonding electrons ‚Üí weaker attraction ‚Üí lower electronegativity.

Ionic Radius

Anions (e.g. S¬≤‚Åª) are larger than the parent atom ‚Äî extra electrons increase electron‚Äìelectron repulsion, expanding the electron cloud.
Cations (e.g. Mg¬≤‚Å∫) are smaller than the parent atom ‚Äî fewer electrons, same nuclear charge pulls them in tighter.

Metallic Character & Group 1

Metallic character increases down a group. Group 1 metals are the most reactive metals.

Reactivity increases down Group 1 because the outer electron is further from the nucleus and more shielded, making it easier to lose.

04 Metallic Bonding & Reactions of Metals ‚ñæ

Metallic Bonding

A lattice of positive metal cations surrounded by a "sea" of delocalised valence electrons.

This model explains metals' key properties:

Malleable/ductile: layers of cations can slide past each other without breaking the bond (delocalised electrons adjust).

Electrical/heat conductors: delocalised electrons move freely through the lattice, transferring charge and energy.

High melting points: strong electrostatic attraction between cations and electron sea requires much energy to overcome.

Reactivity Series & Reactions with Acids

Metals react with acids by losing electrons (oxidation). More reactive metals lose electrons more readily.

Reactivity order from your quiz: Magnesium > Zinc > Copper. Copper does not react with dilute HCl because it cannot displace hydrogen.

General equation: Metal + HCl ‚Üí Metal chloride + H‚ÇÇ

Alkali metals are not found free in nature because they react so readily with water and air.
