⭐ Golden Rules for All Questions
  • Always check: is the response voluntary (operant) or involuntary/reflexive (classical)?
  • For extended response questions: use correct terminology — examiners award marks for NS, UCS, UCR, CS, CR, antecedent, behaviour, consequence, positive/negative reinforcement/punishment.

  • For operant conditioning consequences: name the type AND explain the effect on future behaviour.

Classical Conditioning

Involuntary learning through the repeated association of stimuli. The learner is passive.

🔑 The Five Key Terms
Neutral Stimulus
NS
Initially produces no relevant response. Presented before (or simultaneously with) the UCS. Becomes the CS after conditioning.
Unconditioned Stimulus
UCS
Naturally and automatically produces a response — no learning required. E.g. food, puff of air, electric shock.
Unconditioned Response
UCR
The natural, automatic, involuntary response to the UCS. E.g. salivation to food, blinking to a puff of air.
Conditioned Stimulus
CS
The previously neutral stimulus that now produces a response after repeated pairing with the UCS.
Conditioned Response
CR
The learned, involuntary response to the CS alone. Similar to the UCR. Always directed toward the CS.
📈 The Three-Phase Process
1
Before Conditioning
NS → No response
UCS → UCR

The NS is neutral — it produces no relevant response. The UCS automatically produces the UCR.
→
2
During Conditioning
NS + UCS → UCR
(repeated pairings)

NS is presented immediately before the UCS. The two are repeatedly paired until an association forms. This is acquisition.
→
3
After Conditioning
CS → CR

The NS has become the CS. Presented alone, it produces the CR — a learned, involuntary response, without the UCS being present.
🐕 Pavlov's Classic Example
PhaseStimulusResponse
BeforeBell (NS)No response
BeforeFood (UCS)Salivation (UCR)
DuringBell (NS) + Food (UCS)Salivation (UCR) — repeated many times
AfterBell (CS)Salivation (CR)
📝 Answer Framework — 3-Phase Extended Response
🗂 How to structure a 3-phase answer
1
Name the type of conditioning "This scenario illustrates classical conditioning, a behaviourist approach where learning occurs through the repeated involuntary association of stimuli."
2
Before conditioning: Identify NS and UCS + UCR "Before conditioning, [NS] produces no relevant response in [subject]. [UCS] naturally produces [UCR]."
3
During conditioning: NS before UCS, repeated "During conditioning, [NS] is repeatedly presented immediately before [UCS], which produces [UCR]. This association is formed through repeated pairings."
4
After conditioning: CS produces CR alone "After conditioning (due to acquisition), [CS] alone produces the [CR] — without the UCS being present."
⭐ Classical Conditioning — Things to Remember
  • You MUST include all 5 terms in a 3-phase extended response: NS, UCS, UCR, CS, CR.
  • NS must be presented BEFORE (or simultaneously with) the UCS — never after.
  • The CR is directed toward the CS (e.g. dogs salivate at the bell, not at food).
  • Mention repeated pairings — this is required for full marks.
  • The shorter the time gap between NS and UCS, the faster the learning.
  • Classical conditioning produces INVOLUNTARY responses — the learner is passive.
  • Some conditioning occurs after one trial (e.g. phobias, taste aversion).
⚠️ Common Errors — Classical Conditioning
  • Confusing which is the NS and which is the UCS — identify which stimulus needs NO learning to produce a response (UCS).
  • Forgetting to mention 'repeated associations' during Phase 2 — this costs marks.
  • Saying the learner is 'active' — classical conditioning makes the learner passive.
  • In 2024 Q6b: Omitting 'sound' from the neutral stimulus description — must specify the sound of the electronic bell, not just "the bell."

Operant Conditioning

Voluntary behaviour is shaped by its consequences. Developed by B.F. Skinner in the 1930s.

📋 The ABC Model (Three Phases)
A
Antecedent
The stimulus or environment that precedes the behaviour. Signals the likely consequence. Also called the discriminative stimulus.
→
B
Behaviour
The voluntary action performed by the learner in response to the antecedent. The learner is active.
→
C
Consequence
Occurs immediately after the behaviour. Makes the behaviour more or less likely to occur in the future in the presence of the antecedent.
⚡ The Four Types of Consequences
➕😊
Positive Reinforcement
Desirable stimulus ADDED → Behaviour INCREASES
A desirable stimulus is added after a behaviour, making it more likely to be repeated.
Getting a sticker for staying on task → likely to stay on task again
➖😌
Negative Reinforcement
Undesirable stimulus REMOVED → Behaviour INCREASES
An undesirable stimulus is removed after a behaviour, making it more likely to be repeated.
Taking painkillers removes headache → more likely to take painkillers again
➕😖
Positive Punishment
Undesirable stimulus ADDED → Behaviour DECREASES
An undesirable stimulus is added after a behaviour, making it less likely to be repeated.
Receiving a fine for speeding → less likely to speed again
➖😒
Negative Punishment (Response Cost)
Desirable stimulus REMOVED → Behaviour DECREASES
A desirable stimulus is removed after a behaviour, making it less likely to be repeated.
Phone confiscated for misbehaving → less likely to misbehave again

⭐ Operant Conditioning — Things to Remember
  • Positive = added. Negative = removed. Nothing to do with good/bad.
  • Reinforcement always INCREASES behaviour. Punishment always DECREASES behaviour.
  • The consequence must immediately follow the behaviour to be effective (2024 Q15).
  • Always state: type of consequence + what was added/removed + effect on future behaviour.
  • Antecedent does NOT directly produce the response — the behaviour does (2021 Q10, 57% correct).
  • Stopping/starting a voluntary action (e.g. video games, crossing the road) = operant.
⚠️ Common Errors — Operant Conditioning
  • Reading the question from the wrong person's perspective (e.g. the child's rather than the parent's) — always identify who the question is about.
  • Confusing negative reinforcement with punishment — neg. reinforcement INCREASES behaviour.
  • Only naming "treat" or "fine" as the consequence without labelling the type and effect.
  • Forgetting that after-school detentions are ineffective because there is a delay between behaviour and consequence.

Observational Learning

Learning by watching the behaviour of a model and the consequences of that behaviour. Bandura's social-cognitive approach.

🔁 The Five Stages — ARRM-R
A
Attention
The learner must actively focus on the model. More likely if the model has high status, is similar to, or known by the learner.
Exam tip: Describe what the learner is focusing on and why they would pay attention.
R
Retention
The learner must create a mental representation of the behaviour and remember it. If they can't remember, they can't replicate.
Exam tip: Link to memory consolidation — the learner stores a mental image/representation of the behaviour.
R
Reproduction
The learner must have the physical and mental capability to convert memory into action. May not be able to replicate if lacking strength, coordination, etc.
⚠️ In 2020 VCAA Q4b, reproduction was NOT awarded marks — read questions carefully.
M
Motivation
The learner must want (desire) to reproduce the behaviour. Can be intrinsic (internal desire) or extrinsic (external rewards).
Exam tip: Explain what drives the learner to want to perform the behaviour.
R
Reinforcement
A desirable consequence increases the likelihood of the behaviour. Sources: vicarious reinforcement (watching model rewarded), external reinforcement, or self-reinforcement.
Exam tip: Vicarious reinforcement = seeing someone ELSE rewarded increases your own motivation to perform the behaviour.
👤 Bandura's Research

Albert Bandura (1960s) used his Bobo Doll experiments to show that children who watched adults behave aggressively toward a doll were more likely to display aggression themselves. He emphasised that learning is not automatic — the learner must consciously process and decide to replicate the behaviour (cognitive component).

Type of ReinforcementSourceExample
Vicarious reinforcementObserving the model being rewardedWatching a classmate get praised for answering correctly
External reinforcementRewarded by someone else after performing the behaviourTeacher praises your answer
Self-reinforcementRewarding yourselfFeeling proud after finishing an assignment
⭐ Observational Learning — Things to Remember
  • All five stages must occur for learning to be complete: A → R → R → M → R
  • Behaviour can remain latent (hidden) until needed — immediate replication is NOT required.
  • Bandura emphasised cognitive processes — the learner must consciously decide to replicate.
  • Vicarious punishment (watching someone punished) DECREASES likelihood of that behaviour in the observer.
  • When describing a process, give 1 mark = identify the process + 1 mark = justify with the scenario.

Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander Approaches

A systems approach where learning is embedded in relationships and a multimodal network patterned on Country.

🌿 Key Concepts
Ways of Knowing
Multimodal methods of learning and communicating knowledge through cultural activities (song, dance, art) and practices. Unique, complex, and sophisticated systems different to Western learning.
Country
The whole system of living entities — including landscape, animals, plants, climate, and waterways — in which humans are just a part. All learning is patterned on Country.
Holistic Learning
Involves the whole person — relationships with people, living things, and spirituality. Not broken into separate subjects like Western education.
Multimodal
Using a variety of methods to learn (song, dance, story, art, observation, practice). Enhances memory compared to single-mode Western approaches.
Kinship
Family/community structure that determines who holds knowledge and who has access to it. Sharing knowledge is a person's responsibility. Creates a cohesive, harmonious community.
Systems Approach
Multiple aspects interact and influence each other. Learning is not restricted to teacher-student; the entire community teaches. All elements are inter-related.
🌀 The 8 Ways of Knowing
Story Sharing
We connect through the stories we share.
Learning Maps
We picture our pathways of knowledge.
Non-verbal
We see, think, act, make and share without words.
Symbols & Images
We keep and share knowledge with art and objects.
Land Links
We work with lessons from land and nature.
Non-linear
We put different ideas together and create new knowledge.
Deconstruct/Reconstruct
We work from wholes to parts, watching and then doing.
Community Links
We bring new knowledge home to help our mob.
📊 Western vs Indigenous Learning
FeatureWestern (Written Culture)Aboriginal & TSI (Oral Culture)

Who teachesTeacher → studentsEntire community — everyone teaches and learns
MethodsPredominantly written/verbal (single mode)Multimodal — song, dance, art, story, observation
Relationship to landOften abstracted from environmentDeeply embedded in and connected to Country

⭐ Indigenous Learning — Things to Remember
  • You do NOT need specific examples from particular communities — understand the general system.
  • Key buzzwords for marks: multimodal, holistic, embedded in relationships, patterned on Country, kinship, systems approach.
  • Buzzwords alone are NOT enough — you must answer the question directly.
  • DO NOT say learning is 'a collection of artefacts' — this was explicitly wrong in 2023 VCAA.
  • The systems approach means multiple aspects inter-relate and influence each other — not just one teacher and one subject.

MediumClassical ConditioningExtended Response
Many species of birds can be conditioned to avoid predators by pairing the sound of the predator with an unpleasant stimulus, such as a puff of air. Over time, the birds learn that the sound of the predator alone is dangerous.

Name the behaviourist approach to learning that is discussed in this scenario and outline the three-phase process of conditioning to avoid predators.
2023 VCAA Exam Q3 — 4 marks. Average: 2.3/4
✅ Model Answer (4 marks)
This scenario illustrates classical conditioning, a behaviourist approach where learning occurs through repeated involuntary association between stimuli.
Before conditioning: The sound of the predator (NS) produces no relevant response in the birds. A puff of air (UCS) naturally produces a fear/avoidance response (UCR).
During conditioning: The sound of the predator (NS) is repeatedly presented immediately before the puff of air (UCS), which produces the fear response (UCR). The association is formed through repeated pairings.
After conditioning: Due to acquisition, the sound of the predator alone (CS) now produces a conditioned fear/avoidance response (CR) — without the puff of air being present.

⚠️ Common errors: Mistaking the puff of air as the NS. Not mentioning 'repeated' in Phase 2. Not specifying 'sound of the predator' (just saying 'predator').

5
MediumOperant ConditioningExtended Response
Achara was sharing stories with her son Kris about his childhood. Achara told Kris that every time they visited a supermarket, he would cry for her to buy him chocolates. Achara was able to stop Kris from crying by ignoring his crying and continuing with her shopping.

(a) According to operant conditioning, what were the antecedent, behaviour and consequence for Achara in this story? (3 marks)

(b) What type of consequence for Achara is demonstrated in this story? (1 mark)

(c) With reference to operant conditioning, describe another way Achara could have stopped Kris from crying. (3 marks)
2020 VCAA Exam Q5 — Average: 1.6/3 (Q5a)
✅ Model Answer

(a) From ACHARA's perspective:

Antecedent: Kris (child) cries for chocolate at the supermarket.
Behaviour: Achara (mother) ignores Kris's crying and continues shopping.
Consequence: Kris stops crying — the unpleasant stimulus is removed for Achara.

(b) Type of consequence:

Negative reinforcement — the removal of the unpleasant stimulus (crying) increases the likelihood that Achara will ignore crying in future.

(c) Another way:

Achara could use response cost (negative punishment) — for example, taking Kris's iPad away if he continues to cry for chocolate. The removal of a desirable stimulus (iPad) would decrease the likelihood of Kris crying for chocolate in the future.

⚠️ Most common error: answering from Kris's perspective, not Achara's. Q (c): must use a different type of reinforcement/punishment to Q (b) — don't give another example of negative reinforcement.

6
HardClassical + OperantExtended Response
Toilet-trained cows fighting against climate change

In a recent experiment, researchers conditioned cows to urinate in a pen. In the first stage, all cows were confined to separate pens. Test cows were given a sugar treat after they urinated in the pen. Control cows were given a sugar treat at random times unrelated to what they were doing.

In the second stage, test cows were put outside the pen and given a sugar treat after they went into the pen and urinated. Most test cows learned to urinate in the pen within 15 days. Control cows did not learn.

(a) Identify the independent and dependent variables in the first stage of the research. (2 marks)

(b) Why were control cows used in this research? (1 mark)

(c) Using the language of the appropriate behaviourist approach, explain how the cows learned to urinate in the pen in the second stage. (4 marks)
2023 VCAA Exam Q4 — Average: 1.0/2 (4a), 0.7/1 (4b), 2.3/4 (4c)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Variables in Stage 1:

Independent variable: Whether cows were given a sugar treat immediately after urinating in the pen, or at random times (the timing/contingency of reinforcement).
Dependent variable: Whether the cows learned to urinate in the pen (learning/urination behaviour).

(b) Why control cows:

To establish a baseline and determine whether the sugar treat (reinforcement) — rather than some other variable — was responsible for the cows learning to urinate in the pen. They allow a cause-and-effect relationship to be established.

(c) Stage 2 — Operant Conditioning:

Antecedent: The cow is placed outside the pen / the cow needs to urinate.
Behaviour: The cow moves into the pen and urinates.
Consequence: The cow receives a sugar treat (positive reinforcement — a desirable stimulus is added).
The sugar treat acts as positive reinforcement because it increases the likelihood of the cows returning to the pen to urinate in the future, as they receive a desired stimulus each time they do so.

⚠️ Most common error for (a): naming the two stimuli (torch light and bell) as the IVs instead of the order/timing variable. For (c): must explicitly use operant conditioning terminology (antecedent, behaviour, consequence, positive reinforcement).

HardClassical ConditioningExperiment Design
Pip designed an experiment to examine whether the timing and order of presenting two stimuli affected how quickly classical conditioning occurred. Pip used a torch light (strong stimulus → blink) and an electronic bell. Seven conditions (A–G) were tested with different orders and time delays (0–2 seconds).

(a) List the two independent variables in this experiment. (2 marks)

(b) State the neutral stimulus and unconditioned response. (2 marks)

(c) What would Pip look for to indicate learning had occurred? (1 mark)

(d) Using your knowledge of classical conditioning, predict which condition (A–G) would have the quickest rate of learning. Justify your answer. (3 marks)

Conditions: A–C = torch then bell (0.5s, 1s, 2s); D = simultaneous (0s); E–G = bell then torch (0.5s, 1s, 2s)
2024 VCAA Exam Q6 — Averages: 1.2/2, 1.1/2, 0.3/1, 1.3/3
✅ Model Answer
(a) IV 1: The order of presentation (torch first vs bell first vs simultaneous).
(a) IV 2: The time delay between the two stimuli (0, 0.5, 1.0, or 2.0 seconds).
(b) Neutral stimulus: The sound of the electronic bell. Unconditioned response: Blinking (in response to the torch light).
(c) Pip would look for participants blinking in response to the electronic bell alone (without the torch light) — the bell producing a blink response indicates the CR has formed.
(d) Condition E — bell followed by torch light with a 0.5 second delay — would have the quickest learning. Classical conditioning works best when the NS is presented immediately before the UCS (NS → UCS). Condition E has the NS (bell) before the UCS (torch) with the shortest time delay (0.5s). The short gap between NS and UCS maximises the association formed, while the correct order (NS before UCS) is required for acquisition to occur.

⚠️ Most common error for (a): listing 'torch' and 'bell' as IVs — these are stimuli, not the variables. The IVs are the ORDER and TIME DELAY. For (d): could get 1 mark for selecting A, F, or G with a congruent justification about order or time delay.

Tricky and unusual scenarios to test your ability to apply concepts. Click each card to reveal the analysis.

💊
Classical Conditioning
The Immune Response Drug
Researchers paired a drug that increases immune response (epinephrine) with a placebo (a specific taste). After several pairings, the placebo alone increased immune response. What type of conditioning is this?
Classical Conditioning — the learner is passive, the immune response is involuntary.

• NS: The placebo (specific taste/smell)
• UCS: Epinephrine/drug
• UCR: Increased immune response (to drug)
• CS: Placebo (taste/smell)
• CR: Increased immune response (to placebo alone)

The immune response is involuntary — the body cannot consciously decide to increase its immune response — so this is classical, not operant.
🎮
Operant Conditioning
Can't Stop Playing Video Games
Orla finds it difficult to stop playing her video game. She progresses to higher levels and keeps trying to reach the next one. Is this classical or operant?
Operant Conditioning (A — 31% got this right in 2021 VCAA)

Stopping/continuing to play a video game is a voluntary behaviour — Orla is choosing to keep playing. The positive reinforcement of progressing to higher levels (desirable consequence added) increases the likelihood of continuing to play.

⚠️ Many students chose classical — but there is no involuntary reflexive response here. The key is: is the behaviour voluntary?
🏠
Classical Conditioning
Sarah's Hot House
Sarah keeps her home extremely hot. Every time you visit, you perspire. Last time, you noticed you began perspiring and became uncomfortable as soon as you SAW her house — before even going inside. Classical or operant?
Classical Conditioning — perspiration is involuntary.

• Before: NS (house exterior) → no response | UCS (high temperature) → UCR (perspiration)
• During: Sight of house (NS) repeatedly paired with high temperature (UCS) → perspiration (UCR)
• After: Sight of house (CS) → perspiration (CR) — before even entering

Perspiration in response to heat is a reflexive, involuntary biological response — classical conditioning.
🏠
Operant Conditioning
The Noisy Neighbour
Hannah's landlord sends her an official letter saying her lease will be terminated if she continues hosting loud parties. Hannah reduces her loud parties in response. Which operant conditioning principle is this?
Negative Reinforcement (Answer B — 2018 VCAA)

Hannah is reducing parties to AVOID losing her lease. The threat of lease termination is an unpleasant stimulus — her behaviour (reducing parties) allows her to REMOVE this threat.

• Behaviour: Reducing loud parties
• Consequence: Avoidance/removal of lease termination threat (undesirable stimulus removed)
• Effect: Behaviour (reducing parties) INCREASES

⚠️ This is NOT response cost — nothing has been taken away yet. It is negative reinforcement because she is acting to prevent/remove an undesirable consequence.
🐍
Classical + Operant
Training Dogs to Avoid Snakes
A dog is trained to avoid snakes using two methods: (1) pairing a snake with an electric shock, and (2) giving the dog a treat every time it avoids the snake. Which conditioning type applies to each method?
Method 1: Classical Conditioning
NS: Snake | UCS: Electric shock | UCR: Fear/pain response
→ CS: Snake | CR: Fear response to snake alone

Method 2: Operant Conditioning
A: Snake present (antecedent) | B: Dog avoids snake | C: Treat (positive reinforcement)
→ Increases likelihood of avoiding snake in future

Both forms can work together: classical conditioning creates the involuntary fear response, while operant conditioning shapes the voluntary avoidance behaviour.
🧑‍🍳
Classical Conditioning
Food Aversion After Illness
Mia ate sushi before getting severely ill with food poisoning (unrelated to the sushi). Now she feels nauseous whenever she smells sushi. This happened after only ONE incident. How is this possible in classical conditioning?
Single-Trial Learning / Taste Aversion — a special case of classical conditioning.

• NS: Smell/taste of sushi
• UCS: Severe illness (food poisoning)
• UCR: Nausea (biological response to illness)
• CS: Smell of sushi | CR: Nausea at smell of sushi

Classical conditioning usually requires repeated pairings — but in some cases (particularly involving illness, pain, or strong emotional events like phobias), a single pairing is enough. This is called one-trial learning. The biological significance of avoiding illness makes this association form rapidly.
📱
Operant Conditioning
Wi-Fi Cut Off
Elijah often posts videos when he should be doing homework. When his mother finds out, she turns off his wi-fi, which upsets him. In terms of operant conditioning, this is an example of…
Response Cost / Negative Punishment (Answer A — 2018 VCAA Q19 — 67% correct)

• Behaviour: Posting videos instead of doing homework
• Consequence: Wi-fi access (desirable stimulus) is REMOVED
• Effect: Behaviour DECREASES

This is negative punishment (response cost) — a desirable stimulus (wi-fi/internet access) is taken away, which decreases the likelihood of posting videos during homework time.

⚠️ NOT positive punishment (nothing unpleasant was added). NOT negative reinforcement (nothing removed to increase behaviour).

Indigenous Learning
Learning to Hunt Kangaroo
A young member of an Aboriginal community is learning to hunt kangaroo. They learn not just what weapons to use, but the kangaroo's behaviour, its food sources, predators, seasonal patterns, and the land it inhabits. Why does this reflect a 'systems' approach?
Systems Approach to Learning

This illustrates that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander ways of knowing are a systems approach — multiple aspects of knowledge inter-relate and influence each other:

• The learner is situated within a system (Country, community, ecology)
• Knowledge is holistic — not broken into separate subjects
• Learning is multimodal — through observation, story, practice, community
• Knowledge is patterned on Country — all learning relates to the land
• Community members teach — not just a single teacher-student relationship

This contrasts with Western education where 'hunting' might be studied as separate subjects (biology, geography, physics).

Identify the Consequence + Name its type "The consequence is [specific result]. This is an example of [positive/negative reinforcement/punishment] because [desirable/undesirable stimulus] is [added/removed]."
4
State the effect on future behaviour "As a result, [subject] is [more/less] likely to [repeat the behaviour] in the future."
👁 Framework 3 — Observational Learning (Identify a Process)
Use when asked to: "identify a process of observational learning and justify"
1
Name the process (1 mark) "[Attention / Retention / Motivation / Reinforcement] played a role in [subject]'s learning."
2
Justify with scenario (1 mark) "This is because [specific detail from scenario that shows this process occurring]."
⭐ Process Selection Tips
  • Scenario mentions "watched carefully / focused on" → Attention
  • Scenario mentions "remembered / recalled / created a mental image" → Retention
  • Scenario mentions "wanted to / desired to / was motivated by" → Motivation
  • Scenario mentions "was rewarded / got praise / saw model praised" → Reinforcement
  • Scenario mentions "watched model get punished / avoided behaving that way" → Reinforcement (vicarious punishment)
🌏 Framework 4 — Indigenous Ways of Knowing Response
Use when asked to: "explain how Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander ways of knowing illustrate [concept]"
1
Define the key concept using correct terminology "Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander ways of knowing view learning as [multimodal / holistic / embedded in relationships / patterned on Country / a systems approach]."
2
Explain what this means specifically E.g. for kinship: "The kinship structure determines who holds knowledge and who has access to it. Sharing knowledge is a responsibility to other people and creates a cohesive community."
3
Contrast with Western approaches (if asked) "This differs from Western education, which [separates subjects / relies on individual teacher-student relationships / uses written single-mode techniques]."
⚠️ Indigenous Ways of Knowing — Do NOT say:
  • "A collection of artefacts" — explicitly wrong (2023 VCAA).
  • Only use buzzwords without answering the specific question.
  • That all Aboriginal communities have the same practices — they are diverse.
